Philosophical Interests
I work on issues centering in the philosophy of language and of mind.  I am especially interested in mental states that don't fit the usual philosophical model of propositional thought, and in the expression of such states.  My dissertation was about metaphor. In it, I treat metaphor as a form of speaker meaning which requires the hearer to 'see' one thing 'as' another, or under a certain 'aspect'; I develop a theory of aspectual thought, and explain how the content of the speaker's metaphorical speech act is determined by means of it.  I am currently thinking about how to distinguish semantics from pragmatics, and about the role that aspects play in visual perception.  I am also interested in our engagment with fiction, in the role that emotions play in our cognitive lives, and in the mental representations of animals. 
Background
I finished my PhD in the Philosophy Department at UC Berkeley in the summer of 2003; my advisors were Richard Wollheim, John Searle, and John MacFarlane.  I graduated from the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, in 1993, with a double major in Philosophy and English Literature.  In the years between college and grad school, I worked in Chicago in adult literacy, designing and implementing programs for ESL instruction in the Latino community and for GED instruction in public housing.

Current Papers (in PDF format)

"Contextualism, Metaphor, and What is Said" (forthcoming in Mind and Language)

On a familiar and prima facie plausible view of metaphor, speakers who speak metaphorically say one thing in order to mean another. Several theorists have recently challenged this view; they offer criteria to distinguish what is said from what is merely meant, and argue that these criteria support classifying metaphor within 'what is said'. I consider four such criteria, and argue that when properly understood, they support the traditional classification instead. I conclude by sketching how we might extract a workable notion of ‘what is said’ from our ordinary intuitions about saying and meaning.

"Metaphor and that Certain 'Je Ne Sais Quoi'" (forthcoming in Philosophical Studies)

Contrary to what many proponents of metaphor have claimed, metaphors don't do anything different in kind from what can be done with literal speech. But this does not render metaphor theoretically dispensable or irrelevant, as many analytic philosophers have assumed. In certain circumstances, I argue, metaphors can enable speakers to communicate contents that cannot be stated in fully literal and explicit terms. These cases thus serve as counterexamples to John Searle's 'Principle of Expressibility', the idea that whatever can be meant can be said. Indeed, metaphors can sometimes even provide us with our only cognitive access to certain properties. In the end, thinking about metaphor is useful because it draws our attention to patterns and processes of thought that play a pervasive role in our ordinary thought and talk, and that can genuinely extend our basic communicative and cognitive resources.

"The Generality Constraint, Nonsense, and Categorial Restrictions" (final version in in Philosophical Quarterly 54: 215 (April 2004), pp. 209-231.)

We should not admit categorial restrictions on the significance of syntactically well formed strings. Syntactically well formed but semantically absurd strings, such as 'Life's but a walking shadow' and 'Caesar is a prime number', can express thoughts; and competent thinkers both are able to grasp these and ought to be able to. Gareth Evans' generality constraint, though Evans himself restricted it, should be viewed as a fully general constraint on concept possession and propositional thought. For (a) even well formed but semantically cross-categorial strings often do possess substantive inferential roles; (b) hearers exploit these inferential roles in interpreting such strings metaphorically; (c) there is no good reason to deny truth-conditions to strings with inferential roles.

"Metaphors and Demonstratives: Josef Stern's Metaphor in Context" (forthcoming in Nous)

A discussion of Stern's 2000 book postulating a metaphoricity operator 'Mthat' modeled on Kaplan's 'Dthat'. I focus on Stern's claim that we need to adopt a semantic analysis of metaphor because metaphor exhibits interpretive constraints which cannot be explained on a pragmatic view; I argue that in each case the 'constraint' is merely defeasible, and that a pragmatic analysis can accomodate the data more parsimoniously and in greater generality than Stern's theory can.

"Metaphor" with Marga Reimer (forthcoming in the Handbook of Philosophy of Language, ed. E. Lepore and B. Smith, Oxford UP)

A survey of recent theories of metaphor from the perspective of philosophy of language.

"Making Claims and Seeing-As: A Pragmatic Theory of Metaphor"

(A highly compressed version of my dissertation; I'm planning to write a new, more enlightened version soon-ish.)

After laying out what I take to be the most important features of what metaphor does, I offer my own theory of how metaphor manages to do these things. I argue that metaphor is a pragmatic phenomenon, on which speakers say one thing in order to mean another. What is distinctive about metaphor is that speakers intend for their hearers to recover their intended content by means of cultivating a certain “aspect” for thinking about the subject under discussion. I say how I take “thinking under an aspect” to work, show how the content of a metaphorical speech act is determined on this basis, and draw some consequences from the study of metaphor for philosophical treatments of mental representation more generally.

An abstract of my dissertation, Saying and Seeing-as: The Linguistic Uses and Cognitive Effects of Metaphor
